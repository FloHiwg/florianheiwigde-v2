## Intro

This trip worked because it was not trying to be one single version of Thailand.

Instead, it was built around contrast: Bangkok as the landing point, Chiang Rai and Chiang Mai as the northern culture block, Khao Sok as the nature reset, Koh Yao Yai as the longer island stay, and Singapore as a compact final city stop on the way home.

That made the route feel less like one continuous holiday and more like several smaller trips stitched together in a way that still made sense.

At a high level, the structure looked like this:

- Berlin to Bangkok
- Bangkok
- Chiang Rai
- Chiang Mai
- Khao Sok
- Koh Yao Yai
- Singapore
- Berlin

The route was also a good reminder that variety only works if each stop has a clear role. Bangkok was there to absorb the long-haul arrival and give the trip energy. Northern Thailand added temples, markets, and a slower cultural rhythm. Khao Sok was the rougher nature chapter. Koh Yao Yai was the decompression phase. Singapore worked as a clean urban punctuation mark at the end.

In this article, I want to document the trip stop by stop, including where we stayed, what we did each day, what was worth the effort, and which planning decisions actually made the route work.

## From Berlin to Bangkok

We flew from Berlin to Bangkok with Finnair, leaving on March 10, 2026 and arriving around midday on March 11, 2026.

The first surprise of the trip was that the jet lag felt far easier than expected. That changed the tone of the opening day immediately. Instead of needing a full recovery day, we were able to land, get into the city, check in, and still start the trip properly.

That is one reason Bangkok worked so well as the opener. It gave the route a soft landing without feeling passive. You can arrive tired, keep the first day light, and still get a real sense that the trip has begun.

## Bangkok

### Hotel

For Bangkok, we stayed at Vince Hotel Pratunam from March 11, 2026 to March 14, 2026.

{{< travel-inline-map key="bangkok-base" >}}

This was a practical first-base decision more than a splurge-hotel decision. Bangkok was meant to absorb the long-haul arrival and make the first days easy, so what mattered most was not luxury for its own sake but whether the base made it simple to move around the city.

That part worked. We used the Airport Rail Link and then a tuk-tuk for the final stretch to the hotel, which turned out to be a good preview of how the whole Bangkok section would feel: a little chaotic on paper, but actually very manageable once you were in it.

The area also worked well because there was already enough around the hotel for the first low-energy afternoon. We could check in, walk to a nearby market, eat something, get a foot massage, and then fall into bed early without needing to overthink anything.

The hotel itself felt like the right kind of Bangkok base: good enough to support the trip, but not the reason for the stop. That was the correct tradeoff here because later sections, especially Koh Yao Yai, were where the accommodation itself mattered much more.

### Day by Day

#### March 11, 2026: Arrival in Bangkok

{{< travel-inline-map key="bangkok-base" >}}

We landed around midday, got into the city more smoothly than expected, and were immediately surprised by how gentle the arrival felt. After checking in, we kept the day intentionally light. We walked to a nearby market, ate there, treated ourselves to a foot massage, and then realized halfway through how close we already were to falling asleep.

It was exactly the right kind of opening day. We did not try to force in a sightseeing checklist. Instead, Bangkok introduced itself through heat, traffic, food, and the feeling that the city was moving around us even while we were still running on travel mode.

#### March 12, 2026: Temples, Rooftop Rain Break, Canal Tour, and Chinatown

The first full day was the most classic Bangkok sightseeing day of the trip, and that was a good thing. We started with the major temple and palace area, including the Grand Palace complex and the temple cluster around it. That gave the day the obvious visual anchor it needed.

In the afternoon, a short rain shower pushed us into an equally Bangkok kind of solution: we found a rooftop, had something to eat and drink, and waited it out there. After that, we went down to the pier near the palace area and booked a private canal boat tour on the spot. It was still not cheap, but it was clearly cheaper than the online versions we had seen beforehand, and it was one of the most memorable parts of Bangkok.

The boat took us through the canals, past the Big Buddha, and then dropped us near Wat Arun on the return. Wat Arun was one of the most visually impressive temples of the whole Bangkok section. From there we crossed back by ferry and then tried to make our way home. That final leg was the least elegant part of the day because traffic across Bangkok was predictably messy, but even that turned into a practical learning moment: Grab and Bolt were both useful benchmarks, even when we still ended up using tuk-tuks often.

In the evening, we went to Chinatown and ate our way through the market there. One highlight was a famous pepper soup at a Bib Gourmand spot, but the bigger point was the atmosphere. Bangkok felt energetic without feeling overwhelming, and Chinatown made that especially clear.

#### March 13, 2026: Jim Thompson House, Golden Mount, and One More Market Night

The second full day showed another side of Bangkok. In the morning we visited the Jim Thompson House, which made for a calmer and more curated contrast to the palace-and-temple scale of the day before.

From there we used the local worker ferry to get across for just a few baht and made our way up to Golden Mount. That was one of the most rewarding viewpoints of the Bangkok section. The 360-degree view over the city made it easier to understand the scale of Bangkok, and it also helped explain why the city can feel chaotic at street level but surprisingly coherent once you get above it.

Later we passed through the market near the hotel again, took a break, and then went out one more time in the evening to a more tourist-oriented night market farther away. That market was much more polished than the local one near the hotel, but it was still fun because it offered a huge range of food without much stress. We skipped the famous giant pork-rib towers, which looked slightly absurd in person, and stuck to grilled meat skewers, ribs, roti, and whatever else looked good.

By then Bangkok had fully settled into a pattern that made the city easy to like: a lot of movement, a lot of food, and just enough friction to stay interesting.

### Lessons Learned

- Bangkok is an excellent first stop after a long-haul flight because it gives you plenty to do without forcing you into a rigid schedule on day one.
- Tuk-tuks were far more useful than expected. They were not always the cheapest option, but for two people they often felt worth it because they were fast and easy.
- Use Grab or Bolt as a price anchor before negotiating or accepting a ride. Even if you do not book through them every time, they make it much easier to judge what is reasonable.
- Mix transport modes instead of trying to use only one. AirTrain, ferries, tuk-tuks, and ride-hailing apps all had their place.
- Bangkok did not need more than three nights for this route. It worked because it was a strong opener, not because we tried to do everything.

## From Bangkok to Chiang Rai

On March 14, 2026 we flew from Bangkok to Chiang Rai with Thai Lion Air.

This was the first major mood shift of the route. Bangkok had been dense, fast, and full of moving parts. Chiang Rai was deliberately shorter and simpler. We wanted to include it, but without turning the whole northern Thailand section into an overextended detour.

That one-night decision ended up making sense. Chiang Rai was not supposed to compete with Chiang Mai. It was there to add one distinct day with a different atmosphere, different temple aesthetics, and a more local evening market scene.

## Chiang Rai

### Hotel

For Chiang Rai, we stayed at Mora Boutique Hotel from March 14, 2026 to March 15, 2026.

{{< travel-inline-map key="chiang-rai-base" >}}

The hotel played a very specific role here: not to become a destination in itself, but to make the short stop feel smooth instead of rushed. That part worked well because we arrived early, could use the hotel to reset briefly, and then head straight into the day.

For a one-night stop, that is almost the entire value proposition. You do not need a resort experience. You need something calm, well-run, and easy.

### Day by Day

#### March 14, 2026: White Temple, Blue Temple, Big Buddha, and the Saturday Night Market

We arrived early in Chiang Rai and quickly realized that the stop only really works if you treat it as a focused day rather than a loose city break. The driver who picked us up from the airport offered to take us around for the whole day for 1,000 baht, and that turned out to be one of the best decisions of the northern section.

The main reason was simple: the big sights around Chiang Rai are spread out enough that piecing them together with ad hoc transport would have been annoying. With the driver, the whole day became easy. We went first to the White Temple and the Blue Temple, both of which are clearly more theatrical and tourist-focused than the temple visits in Chiang Mai later on. The White Temple in particular was memorable because it felt both traditional and deliberately strange at the same time, especially once you noticed the modern details and comic-like figures inside.

We also visited the Big Buddha area, where the stronger Chinese influence of the region became much more obvious. That was one of the most interesting parts of Chiang Rai overall. The northern section did not just feel like "more Thailand." It had a visibly different cultural texture.

By evening, Chiang Rai had shifted from sightseeing stop to local market town. Because we were there on a Saturday, we could go to the large night market, which was one of the most charming markets of the whole trip. It felt local rather than built around tourists. There was live music, neighborhood dance groups performing on the square, circle dancing, and an absurd amount of cheap, spicy, very good food. That market alone did a lot to justify Chiang Rai's place in the route.

### Lessons Learned

- One-night stops can work if they add a genuinely different mood instead of just more logistics.
- In Chiang Rai, a private driver for the day was worth it because the main sights are spread out and the pricing was still reasonable.
- The temples around Chiang Rai are visually striking, but they are not a full multi-day program. One focused day was enough.
- The Saturday night market was one of the strongest reasons to include Chiang Rai at all.

## From Chiang Rai to Chiang Mai

On March 15, 2026 we transferred from Chiang Rai to Chiang Mai with a pre-booked car route that included a detour via a tea plantation in the north.

This was one of the better route decisions of the trip because it turned what could have been a pure transfer day into something that still felt distinctive. The tea plantation added a scenic stop, a meal, and a reason not to regret leaving Chiang Rai so quickly.

## Chiang Mai

### Hotel

For Chiang Mai, we stayed at T Heritage Hotel from March 15, 2026 to March 18, 2026.

{{< travel-inline-map key="chiang-mai-base" >}}

Chiang Mai was the longer northern base, so the hotel mattered more here than in Chiang Rai. The location inside the old town worked especially well because it kept the evening market, temples, and general walking routes easy.

The room itself was one of the stronger hotel rooms of the trip. The pool was much less impressive, but that turned out not to matter much because Chiang Mai was not a pool stop anyway. What mattered was having a comfortable room and being within walking distance of the parts of the city we actually wanted to use.

### Day by Day

#### March 15, 2026: Tea Plantation Transfer and Sunday Market Arrival

The drive from Chiang Rai to Chiang Mai already had enough shape to feel like part of the trip rather than dead time. The detour to the tea plantation was a real detour, but it paid off. We drank tea, ate there, and had one more northern stop before moving on.

By the time we reached Chiang Mai in the afternoon, the city felt immediately more settled than Chiang Rai. We checked in, jumped into the pool briefly, and then took advantage of the timing: it was Sunday, which meant the huge Sunday market in the old town was already opening up within walking distance of the hotel.

That market was enormous. It felt like it stretched across the whole evening. The street itself was packed with clothes, souvenirs, and stalls, while many temple courtyards became food courts. There were also bars and jazz spots tucked into the side streets. It was the perfect arrival night because it gave us Chiang Mai's atmosphere without requiring much planning beyond showing up hungry.

#### March 16, 2026: Temple Day in the Old Town

The first full day in Chiang Mai was the classic temple day, and compared with Chiang Rai the tone was different immediately. The temples here felt less like curated spectacle and more like part of the city itself.

We started with the Silver Temple and then continued through other temples in the old town. That made Chiang Mai feel calmer and more walkable than Bangkok. It was still busy, but in a way that felt easier to absorb. Instead of jumping between transport modes all day, the city invited slower movement.

That was one of Chiang Mai's strengths in the route. It gave the trip a more settled rhythm without losing visual interest.

#### March 17, 2026: Nimman Coffee Break and Doi Suthep

On the final full day we saw two more sides of Chiang Mai. In the morning we went to the Nimman area, had coffee there, and spent some time around the mall and surrounding neighborhood. That part of the city looked surprisingly European in places and felt very different from the old town.

Later we went back out from the north gate area and took a taxi up to Doi Suthep. The temple itself was beautiful, but what really made the visit work was the setting above the city. The view over Chiang Mai gave the day a second visual anchor and made the northern section feel complete.

By the end of Chiang Mai, the city had done exactly what it needed to do in the route: it proved that the north was not just about one-night temple hopping, but about settling into a slightly slower and more atmospheric chapter.

### Lessons Learned

- Chiang Mai works best as the real northern base, with Chiang Rai as an optional short contrast stop.
- Staying inside or near the old town is worth it if evening markets and walkability are part of the plan.
- The Sunday market is not something to squeeze in casually. It is big enough to become the main event of the evening.
- The pool mattered much less than the room and location.
- If I planned this route again, I would still split Chiang Rai and Chiang Mai, but only because Chiang Rai stayed intentionally short.

## From Chiang Mai to Khao Sok

On March 18, 2026 we flew from Chiang Mai to Phuket and then continued inland toward Khao Sok.

This was one of the biggest chapter changes of the trip. Northern Thailand had been about markets, temples, and city rhythm. Khao Sok was meant to be the nature chapter, and the transfer effort made that a meaningful test. If the stop did not work, it would feel like a lot of travel for not much return.

The first sign that this section might be rougher was the hotel-organized airport transfer. It worked, but it was expensive, and it set the tone for one of the bigger planning lessons of the whole trip: in Thailand, locally organized transfers over WhatsApp are often far better value than hotel transport.

## Khao Sok

### Hotel

For Khao Sok, we stayed at Phu Siab Mhok@KhaoSok from March 18, 2026 to March 21, 2026.

{{< travel-inline-map key="khao-sok-base" >}}

This was the most mixed hotel experience of the trip.

On paper, the property looked strong. In reality, it felt much rougher than expected. The setting itself was beautiful, with views of the river, mountains, and morning mist. But the room was much more like a simple hut on stilts than the photos had led us to expect. The balcony was hard to use because of the mosquitoes, the bathroom was very basic, and even walking from the room to the lobby meant reaching for mosquito spray first.

That does not automatically make it a bad stay, but it is an important distinction. Khao Sok was the point where "nature" stopped being an aesthetic theme and became a very real part of the experience. That can be great if you want it. It is less great if the photos had made you expect something more polished.

The hotel also had the common downside of isolated nature properties: limited food options and inflated pricing. Dinner had to be pre-ordered early, the food was only okay, and one unpriced mango sticky rice ended up costing more than both main courses combined. That was the clearest value miss of the entire trip.

### Day by Day

#### March 18, 2026: Arrival in Khao Sok

Arriving in Khao Sok felt like a real break with the northern section, but not only in the good ways. The landscape was beautiful immediately, and the view from the property had exactly the kind of river-and-mountain drama we had hoped for. At the same time, the room made it clear that this section would be rougher and more improvised than Bangkok or Chiang Mai.

That tension defined Khao Sok pretty well: beautiful setting, uneven comfort.

#### March 19, 2026: Lake Tour, Kayaks, Jungle Walk, and Cave

{{< travel-inline-map key="khao-sok-day" >}}

We booked a full-day lake tour through the hotel for our first full day. Structurally, it had all the ingredients of a highlight: boat ride, lake scenery, kayaking, food stop, short jungle trek, and cave visit.

The scenery delivered. We started with a boat ride out to a platform where we could use kayaks for about an hour and paddle around the lake, which was one of the more peaceful moments of the Khao Sok stay. Later, after another boat leg, we did a short jungle walk with small waterfalls and streams, which made the day feel more active than just sitting on a tour boat the whole time.

The cave stop was also memorable, if slightly creepy. It was only a short loop, but there were spiders, bats, and snakes, which gave it exactly the kind of low-level tension that makes this sort of stop memorable later.

The problem was not the landscape. It was the tour quality. The group was large, the information from the guide was minimal, and the whole thing had the slightly indifferent feeling that larger shared tours often have. By the time we had spent hours on a hard wooden bench in a small boat, the day felt more tiring than magical.

That still did not make it a mistake. The lake is clearly one of the reasons to come to Khao Sok. But it did make me think that this is one section where paying more for a better-organized or smaller-group version could be worth it.

#### March 20, 2026: Big Tree Trail and Night Safari

The second full day worked better because it mixed two very different experiences.

During the day we hiked the Big Tree trail in the national park directly across from the hotel. The route itself was not especially long, but the heat and humidity made it far more demanding than the distance suggested. That was one of the clearest reminders of the trip that hiking effort in tropical conditions should not be judged by kilometers alone.

In the late afternoon we recovered, ate one more slightly underwhelming hotel dinner, and then went out for a night safari that turned out to be one of the strongest Khao Sok experiences. This time the setup was private, just the two of us with the guide, and that changed everything. Over roughly ninety minutes we saw water snakes, scorpions, frogs, tree snakes, and small nocturnal mammals that looked somewhere between civets and martens. Even more than the sightings themselves, the atmosphere made it work: darkness, noise, and the feeling that the forest had switched into an entirely different mode.

That contrast between the mediocre group lake tour and the very good private night safari says a lot about Khao Sok. The place itself was worth it. The exact format of the activities mattered a lot.

### Lessons Learned

- In Thailand, ask local transport providers on WhatsApp before accepting hotel transfer pricing. It was one of the most reliable money-saving moves of the trip.
- Khao Sok is worth it for the landscape, but comfort levels can differ a lot from what polished booking photos suggest.
- Tropical heat and humidity make even short hikes feel serious.
- Group tours in scenic places are not automatically good tours. In Khao Sok especially, smaller or more private options seem worth prioritizing.
- Nature chapters benefit from clear expectations. This was not luxury nature. It was beautiful, buggy, humid, and sometimes rough.

## From Khao Sok to Koh Yao Yai

On March 21, 2026 we left Khao Sok and made our way to Koh Yao Yai.

This transfer ended up being another planning lesson. Instead of taking the expensive hotel-organized option, we found a local driver via WhatsApp and arranged the ride ourselves. The price came out much better. We paid 2,700 baht to get from the hotel to the pier area near Phuket, then bought the boat tickets separately, and finally arranged a local taxi after arriving on the island.

That whole chain sounds messier than it felt. In practice, it worked smoothly enough and made the arrival at the island hotel feel even more like a payoff chapter after the rougher Khao Sok section.

## Koh Yao Yai

### Hotel

For Koh Yao Yai, we stayed at Santhiya Koh Yao Yai from March 21, 2026 to March 26, 2026.

{{< travel-inline-map key="koh-yao-yai-base" >}}

This was clearly the premium hotel block of the whole trip, and unlike in Khao Sok, the hotel fully understood the assignment.

Arrival already felt different. You reach a parking area first, switch into the hotel's own shuttle pickup, and then enter a property that leans hard into a distinctive teak-wood visual identity. That could easily feel overdone somewhere else, but here it worked. The welcome drink and small cake on arrival were a minor detail, but exactly the kind of minor detail that helps a hotel feel like the reward section of a trip.

The room delivered too. Even without upgrading to one of the private-pool villas, the room still had a sea view and a bathtub on the balcony, which was enough to make the stay feel special. That was the right tradeoff. The premium was already going into the setting and the length of stay. It did not need to become absurd.

The strongest design choice in the route was leaving the longest stay for Koh Yao Yai. After several faster-moving stops, five nights here gave the trip space to slow down properly.

### Day by Day

#### March 21, 2026: Arrival on Koh Yao Yai

The arrival on the island felt like the first time in the whole route that movement stopped being the point. We checked in, settled into the room, and got that immediate confirmation that this was the right section to splurge on.

That matters more than it sounds. Premium stays only feel worth it when the effect is immediate. Here, it was.

#### March 22 and 23, 2026: Resort Days

The first days on Koh Yao Yai were intentionally quiet. We used the hotel, relaxed, walked into town once, bought a few cheaper things from a small local shop, and mostly let the island section do what it was supposed to do: lower the tempo of the trip.

This was also where the difference between a good island hotel and an average one became obvious. The infinity pool was genuinely good, the whole place felt calm, and even practical details like the Wi-Fi held up well enough that it was easy to mix rest with a few work obligations.

#### March 24, 2026: Scooter Day and the Sandbank

{{< travel-inline-map key="koh-yao-yai-days" >}}

One day we rented a scooter and used it to explore more of the island. We had arranged it the evening before so we could leave early, which turned out to be a very good call.

The main goal was the sandbank, and getting there early meant we had one of the strongest low-effort/high-reward moments of the whole trip. We were almost alone, which made the place feel much more special than it probably would have later in the day. After that we continued south, looked at more of the island, saw another beach, and then headed back rather than turning it into a full day on the road.

That was the right decision. Koh Yao Yai was not there to become another checklist destination. The point was to explore just enough to feel the island, then return to the hotel and actually enjoy the reason we had stayed there.

#### March 25, 2026: Private Longtail Tour to the Hong Islands

We had considered booking a full-day island tour through the hotel, but that was exactly the kind of over-programming we wanted to avoid by this stage of the trip. Instead, we asked around on WhatsApp, negotiated a shorter private morning tour without lunch, and ended up with a much better-fit version for 4,500 baht including transfers to the pier.

That turned out to be one of the best decisions of the island stay. The tour took us to the Hong Islands, where the limestone formations and cave-cut coastlines were the visual centerpiece. We also stopped to watch monkeys on one of the smaller islands, and our guide turned out to be excellent, explaining things without turning the morning into a lecture.

Later we stopped at Pak Bia for a short swim and some snorkeling, then continued into Hong Lagoon once the water level was high enough for the boats to enter. That part was beautiful but also the most obviously touristy, simply because every longtail boat follows the same rhythm and lines up in sequence.

We ended at Hong Beach, where it was easy to grab a roti and something cold before heading back. The bigger win, though, was timing. Because we had chosen the shorter version, we were back by midday and could still spend the second half of the day at the pool instead of dragging ourselves through one more all-day excursion.

#### March 26, 2026: Final Slow Morning Before Leaving

One of the smartest planning decisions of the trip was keeping the final island day light before moving on. We had learned from earlier trips that it is much nicer to separate the last big excursion from the next travel day. That way you do not get the feeling of being pushed straight from a tour into the next transfer chain.

It sounds small, but this is exactly the sort of planning decision that changes whether a trip feels elegant or exhausting.

### Lessons Learned

- If an island stay is the premium block of the route, give it enough time to justify itself.
- Santhiya Koh Yao Yai worked because the hotel experience and the room both felt clearly better than the earlier stops.
- You do not need to organize every island day. One scooter day, one boat day, and enough hotel time was the right mix.
- Shorter private tours can be much better than full-day standard tours if the goal is to keep the island section relaxing.
- Leave buffer before the next major transfer. The final empty day on the island was a very good decision.

## From Singapore to Berlin

We flew back to Berlin with Finnair, leaving on March 27, 2026 and arriving on March 28, 2026.

By that point, the route had already moved through several climates, travel speeds, and comfort levels. That is what made the return interesting in hindsight: the trip never felt repetitive, but it also proved that variety only works when the transitions are handled well.

## Overall Lessons Learned

- This route worked because each stop had a clear role instead of trying to do the same thing in slightly different ways.
- Bangkok was a strong opener because it absorbed the long-haul arrival without wasting time.
- Chiang Rai and Chiang Mai worked well together only because Chiang Rai stayed very short and Chiang Mai acted as the real northern base.
- Khao Sok was worth it for the scenery, but it was also the chapter that needed the most realistic expectations on comfort and tour quality.
- Koh Yao Yai was the payoff section, and giving it the longest stay was one of the best decisions of the whole route.
- In Thailand, local transport arranged directly, often via WhatsApp, can be far better value than hotel-organized transfers.
- The trip benefited from not packing the last day of every stop. Recovery days and buffer days made the whole route feel much calmer.

## Final Verdict

On paper, this was one Thailand trip with a Singapore add-on. In practice, it felt more like five different travel moods connected in the right order: Bangkok energy, northern Thailand culture, Khao Sok nature, island decompression, and a final city exit.

That is why the route worked. Not every single stop was equally polished, and Khao Sok in particular had more friction than the rest. But the trip was strong because it kept changing shape at the right time.

If I were planning it again, I would keep the overall structure almost exactly as it was: Bangkok first, a short Chiang Rai, Chiang Mai as the northern base, Khao Sok with better-managed expectations, Koh Yao Yai as the splurge section, and then a final city night before flying home.
